The Islamic Era

In 647 CE, the Muslim forces launched their first campaign in the region known as Ifriqiya, marking the start of the Islamic influence in Tunisia. The early campaigns, led by Abdullah ibn Saad, defeated Byzantine forces, culminating in the capture of Carthage in 698 CE. The region became a base for further Islamic expansions, notably into Spain by Berber commanders like Tariq ibn Ziyad.

The Routes of Islamic Expansion

Kairouan: A Center of Knowledge


Scholars from Kairouan contributed to various fields, including theology, mathematics, and medicine.

Kairouan became a prominent center of learning and culture. The Great Mosque of Kairouan, constructed in the 7th century, symbolizes Tunisia's role in Islamic scholarship. The city played a critical role in propagating the Maliki school of Islamic law, influencing legal and religious practices across North Africa.The establishment of Ez-Zitouna University in 737 CE, which became a beacon of Arabic and Islamic education.

The Dynasties of Islamic Tunisia


Tunisia flourished under various Islamic dynasties, each leaving its mark:
* Aghlabids (800-909 CE): Renowned for their architectural achievements, including the Aghlabid Basins, which served as water reservoirs for Kairouan.
* Fatimids (909-973 CE): Established Mahdia as their capital, spreading Shiite Islam before moving their seat to Cairo.
* Hafsids (1228-1574 CE): Based in Tunis, they oversaw a golden age of trade and cultural exchange, solidifying Sunni Islam and Arabic as dominant forces.

Cultural Legacy of the Islamic Era

The Islamic period deeply influenced Tunisian culture. Arabic became the dominant language, blending with Berber traditions. Islamic art and architecture flourished, as seen in the intricate designs of Tunisian mosques and madrasas. The era also shaped Tunisia's culinary traditions, introducing spices and techniques from across the Islamic world.
Cultural Integration: The fusion of Arab and Berber cultures created a unique Tunisian identity.
Enduring Landmarks: Historic sites like the Zitouna Mosque in Tunis and the medina of Sousse reflect this era's legacy.
